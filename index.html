Housing Eviction and Violence

A harsh reality for people who inject drugs in Vancouver, Canada

"Eviction doesn't just precede homelessness—it amplifies the risk of violence."

In Vancouver, people who inject drugs (PWID) face a significant risk of violence. Recent findings reveal that residential eviction further increases the likelihood of violence—regardless of gender. This webpage explores the critical link between losing housing and experiencing harm within this vulnerable population.

The Adjusted Odds Ratio (AOR) is a statistical measure that shows the relationship between a specific factor—such as residential eviction—and an outcome—like experiencing violence, while controlling for other variables that may affect the result.

In Kennedy’s study, the AOR was calculated by accounting for potential confounding factors, such as involvement in sex work, experiences of police violence, and HIV status. These factors themselves may also influence the risk of violence, so adjusting for them helps reveal a clearer and more accurate association between eviction and violence.

Why It Matters

Health & Social Implications

  • The constant threat of violence can force drug use behaviors further underground, increasing the transmission risks for HIV and hepatitis C.
  • It can also trigger or worsen mental health conditions like anxiety, depression, and PTSD.
  • For women, eviction often places them in even more precarious situations, exposing them to increased violence from acquaintances, partners, or strangers.

Policy & Service Gaps

  • The study underscores the vital need for stable and affordable housing for marginalized groups. Simply targeting "homelessness" doesn't adequately address the acute challenges faced by those on the brink of eviction.
  • Multi-agency collaboration is crucial—linking housing policy, mental health services, anti-violence interventions, and harm reduction support.

What Should We Do?

Addressing this complex issue requires a multi-faceted approach:

Secure & Affordable Housing

Prioritize policies that prevent eviction and increase access to safe, stable housing options.

Expand Harm Reduction

Enhance services that reduce the risks associated with drug use and provide support without judgment.

Strengthen Community Support

Build networks that offer social, emotional, and practical assistance to those facing instability.
